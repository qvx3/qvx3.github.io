Based on the above tables, it can be concluded that the spelling reflects the pronunciation of words to an equal or greater extent than it was previously. The letter i is sufficient to indicate the palatalization of the previous one only if there is a consonant after it. Unification of the value of letter i after the consonant is a secondary part of the spelling changes, which by no means will bring benefits such as their original part. The pronunciation of sounds as voiced or voiceless deliberately still does not result directly from spelling, because it facilitates the inflection of words. Next, inflected words will always be written according to their etymology; the spelling of non-inflected words results from the pronunciation of related words and even their equivalents in other languages ​​belonging to one family.

Each of the letters q, v, x modifies only one previous sound: q pulls back the place of articulation, v forms a relation to a semi-vowel (dv is an exception), and x softens the consonant. None of these three letters has had or would receive a new phonetic value in native words or loanwords with spelling adapted to pronunciation, because they were not present in any of these. Such use of these letters or the elimination of diacritical signs from spelling should not be blocked due to aesthetic considerations or an attachment to current word shapes. However, these three modifying letters do not replace diacritical marks found in quotes in a foreign language; hardware-based inability to enter a diacritized letter prompts one to use a letter without such a character. This also applies to inclined vowels used in Polish dialects and in old literature.

Any Latin computer keyboard layout, just like the QWERTY commonly used in Polish country, will still be enough to write Polish. Nevertheless, this layout is not ergonomic and it is recommended to eliminate it. Below is an arrangement presented on Klawa.pl website, but modified for new use of the letters q, v, x. The assumptions accompanying the development of a character arrangement different from QWERTY were to reduce fatigue of the fingers and joints, relieving the wrists, speeding up the process of entering text after the user acquired non-sighted writing skills, and – which is the least important – easier access to letters of the Polish alphabet.
