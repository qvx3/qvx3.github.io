18th June 2021

8. Author

1. Who are you?
My name is Krystian Sebastian Sławiński (krystjan sebastjan slvawinxski). I graduated from electronic technical school at the Zespół Szkół nr 9 im. Romualda Traugutta w Koszalinie, and continue education to become an engineer of automated systems. I have been learning English for 16 years and German for 7 years. Joining the University of Technology I also started attending its choir conducted by prof. Radosław Wilkiewicz, because playing the keyboard instruments at home was not sufficient for me.

2. How did you prepare for such a manifesto?
On the 4th of November, 2017 I started translating from English to Polish on the Falstad.com/circuit/polish website, which has a program simulating electronic circuits. I was motivated by gratitude to author of the program resulting from my successes in learning electronics, as well as the fact that for over four years Polish users have been holding the second place in terms of the number of page views. While updating the translation – citing the preface of my website – 'I encountered problems related to displaying Polish letters: when the source code of a website does not indicate a code page, an Internet browser replaces these letters with incorrect characters or removes them from the text.'

3. Did you seek support?
On the 5th of April, 2018 I sent a letter with The proposed changes in contemporary Polish spelling to the Minister of National Education Anna Zalewska. My personal willingness to teach and the Ministry's lack of response have prompted me to act independently in this matter.

4. How to introduce the changes?
Currently, I suggest that my proposal of changes in spelling should come into force immediately, after all, the writing system is not going to change. Graduation maybe allows to extract all their benefits. According to Irena Sławińska, my grandmother born in 1937, people in post-working age are able to learn new spelling rules.

5. What shall not change that much in the language?
Throughout her life, Irena has learned to recite and sing countless Polish and many Russian lyrical works, so simplifying spelling will not ruin this ability - excellent recitation and singing are independent of the writing. The new rules are designed to facilitate learning in various fields and improve the quality of communication of Poles in their native language. My peers' lack of care for linguistic correctness is partly due to the imperfection of technology, and this reform gives a chance to correct it.
