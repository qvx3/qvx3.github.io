The proposed changes in contemporary Polish spelling

1. Foreword

I would like to present my proposal of changes in the spelling of Polish language. I encountered problems related to displaying Polish letters: when the source code of a website does not indicate a code page, an Internet browser replaces these letters with incorrect characters or removes them from the text. I admire my national tongue with all my heart, so I was thoroughly looking for a solution to such difficulties so that it would be in harmony with the past and future times. Exploring works of Grzegorz Jagodziński, prof. Bronisław Rocławski and Marcin Kowalczyk, I came to my own conclusion that ensuring the writing system's compliance with digital communication standards will bring the most benefits.

Let us strive for changes with consciousness and strive collectively. The voice of a people influences response of the world, so all nations can adapt to ours. Nowadays intellectual progress is dependent on profit like never before. Let us work in this field of our own language, and the rest will accept it. In this way, we will also make it easier for the world to learn about our culture.

Benefits of the change

  1. Greater [see 3. Tables, 5. Example]
    • Distinction of consonant clusters rz, dz from consonants written with the same letters.
    • Facilitation for people with dyslexia by reduction of acutes and dots marking different letters.
    • Less loss of pronunciation and meaning data of words in the case of deliberate omission of modifiers q, v, x, as also diacritics are omitted in typing.
  2. Lesser
    • Easier connecting letters if the use of dots and acutes above letters shall be completely abandoned.
    • Hence, a higher probability to write in the dark while staying the row lines.
    • Connection with the Polish language's history, if only because of keeping distinct letter pairs: rz, ż as rq, zq.
    • The spelling rules' name (referred to as QVX), according to the Classical Latin script, shall be pronounced short as /kuks/, /cooks/, /kuchs/, /cux/ in Polish, English, German or Spanish language respectively.
