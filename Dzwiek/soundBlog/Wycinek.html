5. Keyboards Amateur

This is a nickname from 2020 for my musician role. As an electronics passionate since 2014, I am strongly keen on game soundtracks, eg. multiplayer role-playing fantasy for personal computers. Although I hardly ever listen to songs (especially these over radio), singing is my most frequent musical activity as a bass in the Koszalin University Choir. This time I am going to describe why I decided to publish my performance and modifications of musical works, aligned from older to newer on my SoundCloud profile. Most of them suit well pipe organ besides digital grand piano, or simply the instruments with a pedalboard besides manuals. I promise writing more about my digital piano next time.

1. Donkey Kong Country – Aquatic Ambiance. I wished to play it aggressively, for I managed to apply such style on it in c minor, and recorded about 27th September 2019. Originally the steady rhythm enhanced by base slowly gaining force and losing it like a long wave. It was a brilliant track bringing a listener underwater, where the player learns a totally different environment than before. Supposedly, Donkey Kong Country 2 is more popular than the first entry in Country chapter of the series.

2. Wario Land II – Syrup Castle. One of my favorite video game series consists of this one, not only due to its soundtrack. A drastically different approach to a platformer by Nintendo lets the player enjoy Wario Land II without Game over or the main character getting brought to the beginning of a long level. All it forces less anxiety than Donkey Kong Country gameplay. The Syrup Castle consists only of wooden reed-instruments with strong crossover distortion effect. After all, the sounds resemble oboe with addition of grand piano. The timbre of instruments here is kind of disguised also due to reverb applied. Too bad the composer Kozue Ishikawa had to fill such an excellent video game series with many tracks based on scarce amount of themes; next Wario Land III is even less diverse than its predecessor.

3. Runescape – Dark. One of the easiest melodies from my playlist in minor scales a, d. The composition by Ian Taylor ends with his another track called Intrepid but I chose to split this part in two and drop the second one. It is too tedious, far too different from the former fragments. My performance varies in speed as a replacement, to leave Dark still long enough. On the other hand, the rest of Intrepid introduced pizzicato technique to the melody, which I took instead of french horns in whole musical piece. Luckily enough, the in-game music within Old School Runescape consists of 415 MIDI files. Alas, in my opinion this game (excluding music) got ruined by its creators when much different than fantasy content was being added.

4. Waldemar Kazanecki – Dom (Main Theme). Surprisingly, this music comes from the infro of a Polish television series which name translates to Home. In fact, I have been learning it from score unlike most of music I play, before completing the fifth year of piano music course as a child. Now, I chose virtual pipe organ to record Dom for the sake of more variation in the timbres instead of just one piano at the cost of dynamics. I was not sure if one long dissonance added to its first part could hurt much the overall style, but it can attract back the attention of a listener.

5. Nethack Falcon's Eye – Shopping. Possibly a too calm version of the joyful welcome from the remake of a crude role-playing game set in fantasy genre, from the Nethack Falcon's Eye. The effect in the second half (repeated first one) of the track is called wah-wah, appearing in guitar music. The Falcon's Eye has been created by Jaakko Peltonen, a talented Finnish man who was giving it both graphics and music between 1999 and 2001, however former editions of the Nethack were mute. The name of Nethack comes from its source code availability in the worldwide USENET computer communication technology, in contrast to both predecessors Hack, Rogue closed-source games. Back then, such a downloadable game had to be as small as possible, with text-based illusion of graphics and no sound at all.

6. Lands of Lore II – City of Ancients. It introduced an improvement to my interpretation of computer game music, leading to start of a Youtube channel. QWERTY-keyboard performance of this track, captured on 30th March 2021 by my mother Ewa, appeared online almost three months later. All had to wait until our summer holiday. This game was highly appreciated by the Germans who were involved in creating expansion for Clonk Rage with more realistic industry; Lands of Lore II MIDI files were shipped within this expansion. I was fascinated by Clonk Rage between 2013-2017 due to how my neighbor Paweł used to play it with his sister Anna.

6. Yamaha CLP-220 weighted-key MIDI controller

Although I already had 2.1 loudspeaker system Creative A200 for desktop computer sound and music, the secondary system was needed in order to enhance tone produced by Yamaha CLP-220 digital grand piano. As an amateur organist, I enjoy detuning the internal audio generator of this 88-key synthesizer. When it sounds like a pipe organ, an additional computer software like Miditzer Style 216 also produces organ tones but tuned at 440Hz. Two speakers in front of me and two behind are able to create an illusion of organ celeste as with Vox coelestis pipes. This phenomenon comes to life due to interference of sound waves slightly differing in frequency.

The CLP-220 has a tiny amount of presets, here named by me: 00 Grand Piano 1, 01 Grand Piano 2, 02 Rhodes 1, 03 Rhodes 2, 04 Harpsichord 1, 05 Harpsichord 2, 06 Vibraphone 1, 07 Pipe Organ 1, 08 Pipe Organ 2, 09 Bowed Ensemble 1. According to the manual, 00 has been recorded throughout its full scale in order to be implemented as PCM signal, whereas all other presets are completely synthetic. Strangely enough, not 00 (nor 01) but 03 differs in timbre depending on a key hit-velocity. Actually, not only Rhodes but real grand pianos behave like 03. On the other hand newer Yamaha pianos like DGX-640 (which I sometimes play), take advantage of much more virtual instruments than mine, even if many of them were not recorded in their full scale. As a digital grand piano, CLP-220 comes with dedicated three pedals and a pair of stereo 1/4" jacks underneath elegant wooden case.

I constructed an analog effect circuit consisting of four point-contact diodes (supposedly AAP153 germanium diodes of low forward voltage drop), producing crossover distortion if their load is around 32Ω each of stereo channels. The circuit has an output to two 3.5mm jacks: one for headphones, second for high-impedance device which can capture or simply amplify the distorted signal.

The diodes are connected in parallel but flipped, therefore each pair act as a nonlinear resistor: highly attenuate weak signals and try to pass stronger. At low current draw – when connected to external amplifier only – the distortion is not noticeable. The higher current from CLP-220 through these diodes, the more distortion occurs. As a result, this diode-based effect could be concerned as a passive signal-power filter whereas typical signal processing techniques exploit also frequency filters. This power filter gives a better effect if lower impedance is connected to its output, contrary to passive frequency filters which need high impedance load.

The less polyphony a user decides to have in their performance, the more clear the result of distortion is and the timbre lacks unpleasant cracking. But if rather a very complex waveform approaches the diode circuit's input, eg. 09 Bowed Ensemble, even the perfect intervals can sound bad due to aforementioned cracking.

As a marginal note, the main drawback of Creative A200 is unstable 9-pin mini-DIN connection of its subwoofer to a speaker with volume control. Cable twisted nearby the plug or simply partial connection resulted in no sound but only 50Hz hum and cracking. The cable needed wooden sticks to be stuck by its plug to prevent this unwanted sound. Sadly, Creative Labs used to prefer mounting 9-pin mini-DIN connectors in audio equipment such as 2.0 loudspeakers GigaWorks T40, and the plugs are prone to sticking out.
